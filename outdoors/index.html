Long before the websites

Outdoors.

Before the engineering degree, before the ZAO, before a single line of code: 10 years on ski patrol and an Eagle Scout rank. Most of what I know about running a team, staying calm under stress, and finishing what I start came from up there - not from any office.

Eagle Scout, 2016

The rank takes about seven years if you take it seriously. I earned it in 2016 - 21 merit badges including the required Personal Management, Family Life, Communication, Cooking, First Aid, Emergency Preparedness, Environmental Science, and Citizenship in the Community / Nation / World. The Eagle project itself is an exercise in coordinating other people: scope a community problem, build a plan, recruit volunteers, get it shipped, document everything.

The skill I keep using from Scouts is the discipline of working with adults as a kid - showing up on time, writing the email, asking for what you need. It's the same skill as running a Farcaster channel or a ZAO governance meeting today, just with more sap on your hands.

National Ski Patrol - 2014-present

Eleven seasons. Three mountains. The job is straightforward: ski the hill, watch the lifts, ski to people who fell, splint or sled them down, write the report. The hard part is what nobody tells you - it is mostly waiting in cold weather, then suddenly executing a high-stress sequence with no rehearsal, then writing it all up. Calmly.

2014-2022
Blue Hills, Massachusetts Original home patrol. Trained up through OEC (Outdoor Emergency Care). Learned how to ski with a 200lb sled behind you in 3 inches of new snow. Worked in the same patrol room as people who had been doing it for 30 years - you absorb a lot just by listening.
2022-2024
Mt Hood, Oregon Two seasons of West Coast snow. Wider terrain, longer days, weather that changes mid-shift. Cross-trained with a much larger patrol team. The Pacific Northwest snow taught me how to read a hill when you can't see the bottom.
2025-present
Hermon Mountain, Maine Current home patrol. Tighter community, smaller hill, but the same job. Back on the East Coast for the rest of the work I do here in Maine.

Why this matters for the work

When a client emails at 2am because something broke, or a Farcaster channel I run melts down because of a misconfigured cron, or a co-founder needs me to take over a meeting with 90 seconds notice - the response is the same one I learned in a patrol room. Slow your breathing. Triage. Do the next correct thing. Write it up afterward. That is the whole skill.
